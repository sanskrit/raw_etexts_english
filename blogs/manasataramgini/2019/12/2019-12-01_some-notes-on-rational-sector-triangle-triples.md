**Rational points on a unit circle**  
There are some events that happen in the course of ones life that might
be considered historical or world-changing. One such event from our
lifetime is the proving of the Last Theorem of Fermat (FLT). While the
proof of the FLT is inaccessible to persons with meager mathematical
understanding like us, its algebraic form can be understood by anyone
with a very limited knowledge of mathematics. Its geometric form is
deeply linked to a basic aspect of Euclidean space, and it was this form
that amazed us as a kid. This can be presented as a very simple problem:
given the curve ![x^n+y^n=1; \\; n=1, 2,
3,...](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%5En%2By%5En%3D1%3B+%5C%3B+n%3D1%2C+2%2C+3%2C...&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x^n+y^n=1; \\; n=1, 2, 3,...") (Figure 1), are there rational points
that lie on the curve? A rational point is a point
![(x,y)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28x%2Cy%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(x,y)") where both
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "x")
and ![y](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y") are rational numbers.

[![Fermat\_LT\_curves](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/12/fermat_lt_curves.png?w=554&h=466)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/12/fermat_lt_curves.png)Figure
1

These curves may be divided two topologically distinct groups based on
the parity of the exponent
![n](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "n").
If ![n=2k+1; k=0, 1,
2...](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D2k%2B1%3B+k%3D0%2C+1%2C+2...&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=2k+1; k=0, 1, 2...") then the curve is bilaterally symmetric about
![y=x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y%3Dx&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y=x") and cuts the x-axis and the y-axis once at
![(1,0)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%281%2C0%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(1,0)") and
![(0,1)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%280%2C1%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(0,1)") respectively (Figure 1). If instead ![n=2k+2; k=0, 1,
2...](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D2k%2B2%3B+k%3D0%2C+1%2C+2...&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=2k+2; k=0, 1, 2...") then the curve is closed, symmetric about both
axes and the lines ![y = \\pm
x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y+%3D+%5Cpm+x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y = \\pm x"), and cuts the the x-axis and the y-axis twice at ![(\\pm
1,
0)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28%5Cpm+1%2C+0%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(\\pm 1, 0)") and ![(0, \\pm
1)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%280%2C+%5Cpm+1%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(0, \\pm 1)") respectively (Figure 1). It is easy to see that the case
where
![n=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=1") has infinite rational points. The case for
![n=2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=2") defines a circle and that too has infinite rational points on it
(see below). Remarkably, the FLT states that there are no rational
points for any of the curves with ![n \\ge
3](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n+%5Cge+3&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n \\ge 3"), other than of course the trivial points where the curve
cuts the axes. This is a rather profound statement regarding the
geometry of Euclidean space as it means that, other than the line
![x+y=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%2By%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x+y=1") and the unit circle, none of other members of this family of
unit curves can ever pass through non-trivial rational points.

Now, what are the rational points on a circle? These are linked to the
defining theorem of Euclidean space — the theorem of the right triangle
or the bhujā-koṭi-karṇa-nyāya: we have for a right triangle with the
three sides ![a, b,
c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a%2C+b%2C+c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a, b, c") in order of length,  
![a^2+b^2=c^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a%5E2%2Bb%5E2%3Dc%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a^2+b^2=c^2")

Dividing the equation by
![c^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"c^2") we get:  
![\\dfrac{a^2}{c^2}+\\dfrac{b^2}{c^2}=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdfrac%7Ba%5E2%7D%7Bc%5E2%7D%2B%5Cdfrac%7Bb%5E2%7D%7Bc%5E2%7D%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\dfrac{a^2}{c^2}+\\dfrac{b^2}{c^2}=1")

Writing
![\\tfrac{a}{c}=x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7Bc%7D%3Dx&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{a}{c}=x") and
![\\tfrac{b}{c}=y](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Bb%7D%7Bc%7D%3Dy&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{b}{c}=y") we get the equation of the unit circle:  
![x^2+y^2=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%5E2%2By%5E2%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x^2+y^2=1")

Therefore, the primitive right-triangle triples
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") (because they are
![90^{\\circ}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=90%5E%7B%5Ccirc%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"90^{\\circ}") triangles), where
![\\textrm{GCD}(a,b,c)=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctextrm%7BGCD%7D%28a%2Cb%2Cc%29%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\textrm{GCD}(a,b,c)=1") define the rational points on a unit circle of
the form ![\\left( \\tfrac{a}{c},
\\tfrac{b}{c}\\right)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cleft%28+%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7Bc%7D%2C+%5Ctfrac%7Bb%7D%7Bc%7D%5Cright%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\left( \\tfrac{a}{c}, \\tfrac{b}{c}\\right)"). It has been known since
antiquity that the right triangle triples have a deep connection to the
greatest common divisor of two numbers. Thus, given two positive
integers ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v"), if
![\\textrm{GCD}(u,v)=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctextrm%7BGCD%7D%28u%2Cv%29%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\textrm{GCD}(u,v)=1") and
![v\>u](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=v%3Eu&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"v\>u"), we can generate triples using the below formulae (something one
learns by secondary school):

![x=v^2-u^2\\\\\[6pt\] y=2uv\\\\\[6pt\]
z=u^2+v^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%3Dv%5E2-u%5E2%5C%5C%5B6pt%5D+y%3D2uv%5C%5C%5B6pt%5D+z%3Du%5E2%2Bv%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x=v^2-u^2\\\\[6pt] y=2uv\\\\[6pt] z=u^2+v^2")

For example, ![u=1,
v=2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D1%2C+v%3D2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=1, v=2") yield ![3, 4,
5](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=3%2C+4%2C+5&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"3, 4, 5"), the most primitive of all
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples. It figures in many ancient holy objects like the
Śrīcakra or in the layout of the city of Ayodhyā. However, if both
![u, v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") are odd then
![v^2-u^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=v%5E2-u%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"v^2-u^2"),
![2uv](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=2uv&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"2uv") and
![u^2+v^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%5E2%2Bv%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u^2+v^2") will all be even. Hence, they cannot constitute a primitive
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triple: e.g. ![u=1,
v=3](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D1%2C+v%3D3&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=1, v=3") will yield ![6, 8,
10](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=6%2C+8%2C+10&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"6, 8, 10") which is simply the ![3, 4,
5](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=3%2C+4%2C+5&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"3, 4, 5") triangle magnified 2 times. Thus, we need an additional
condition so that we consider only those triples generated by the above
formulae which themselves have ![\\textrm{GCD}(v^2-u^2, 2uv,
u^2+v^2)=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctextrm%7BGCD%7D%28v%5E2-u%5E2%2C+2uv%2C+u%5E2%2Bv%5E2%29%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\textrm{GCD}(v^2-u^2, 2uv, u^2+v^2)=1"). Thus, we eliminate all ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") sharing an odd parity. Given that the above procedure ensures
that
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") will be positive, if we further ensure that the sides are
arranged by increasing order of length then the minimal set of primitive
triples, ![a, b,
c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a%2C+b%2C+c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a, b, c"), will necessarily occupy only the octant of a circle between
![\\left\[\\tfrac{\\pi}{4},\\tfrac{\\pi}{2}\\right\]](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cleft%5B%5Ctfrac%7B%5Cpi%7D%7B4%7D%2C%5Ctfrac%7B%5Cpi%7D%7B2%7D%5Cright%5D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\left[\\tfrac{\\pi}{4},\\tfrac{\\pi}{2}\\right]") (Figure 2). This set
of primitive triples
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") can then be used to get rational points on the entire
perimeter of the circle by placing
![b](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=b&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "b")
before ![a](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a") and changing their signs one at a time.  
[![triples\_bbk\_below10000](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/12/triples_bbk_below10000.png?w=518&h=518)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/12/triples_bbk_below10000.png)

Figure 2. ![T\_{90} \\le
10000](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D+%5Cle+10000&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90} \\le 10000")

The above procedure shows that there are infinite
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples (Figure 2 shows those with ![c \\le
10000](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c+%5Cle+10000&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"c \\le 10000")) and thus there are infinite rational points that lie on
a unit circle. However, for the past 80 or so years it has been known
that there is a hierarchy to the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples with the ![T^0\_{90}=(3, 4,
5)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T%5E0_%7B90%7D%3D%283%2C+4%2C+5%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T^0_{90}=(3, 4, 5)") triple being the mother of all of them. We
represent it as the below matrix:

![T^0\_{90}=\\begin{bmatrix} 3\\\\ 4\\\\ 5\\\\
\\end{bmatrix}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T%5E0_%7B90%7D%3D%5Cbegin%7Bbmatrix%7D+3%5C%5C+4%5C%5C+5%5C%5C+%5Cend%7Bbmatrix%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T^0_{90}=\\begin{bmatrix} 3\\\\ 4\\\\ 5\\\\ \\end{bmatrix}")

We then define 3 transformation matrices:

![M\_1= \\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 \\\\ 2 & 1 & 2 \\\\ 2 & 2 & 3 \\\\
\\end{bmatrix}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_1%3D+%5Cbegin%7Bbmatrix%7D+1+%26+2+%26+2+%5C%5C+2+%26+1+%26+2+%5C%5C+2+%26+2+%26+3+%5C%5C+%5Cend%7Bbmatrix%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"M_1= \\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 \\\\ 2 & 1 & 2 \\\\ 2 & 2 & 3 \\\\ \\end{bmatrix}")

![M\_2= \\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 & 2 \\\\ 2 & -1 & 2 \\\\ 2 & -2 & 3 \\\\
\\end{bmatrix}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_2%3D+%5Cbegin%7Bbmatrix%7D+1+%26+-2+%26+2+%5C%5C+2+%26+-1+%26+2+%5C%5C+2+%26+-2+%26+3+%5C%5C+%5Cend%7Bbmatrix%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"M_2= \\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 & 2 \\\\ 2 & -1 & 2 \\\\ 2 & -2 & 3 \\\\ \\end{bmatrix}")

![M\_3= \\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 & 2 \\\\ -2 & 1 & 2 \\\\ -2 & 2 & 3 \\\\
\\end{bmatrix}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_3%3D+%5Cbegin%7Bbmatrix%7D+-1+%26+2+%26+2+%5C%5C+-2+%26+1+%26+2+%5C%5C+-2+%26+2+%26+3+%5C%5C+%5Cend%7Bbmatrix%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"M_3= \\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 & 2 \\\\ -2 & 1 & 2 \\\\ -2 & 2 & 3 \\\\ \\end{bmatrix}")

Then we get three further generation-1 triples from
![\\mathbf{T^{0}\_{90}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathbf%7BT%5E%7B0%7D_%7B90%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\mathbf{T^{0}_{90}}") thus:

![\\mathbf{T^{1.1}\_{90}=M\_1 \\cdot T^0\_{90}; \\; T^{1.2}\_{90}=M\_2
\\cdot T^0\_{90}; \\; T^{1.3}\_{90}=M\_3 \\cdot
T^0\_{90}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathbf%7BT%5E%7B1.1%7D_%7B90%7D%3DM_1+%5Ccdot+T%5E0_%7B90%7D%3B+%5C%3B+T%5E%7B1.2%7D_%7B90%7D%3DM_2+%5Ccdot+T%5E0_%7B90%7D%3B+%5C%3B+T%5E%7B1.3%7D_%7B90%7D%3DM_3+%5Ccdot+T%5E0_%7B90%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\mathbf{T^{1.1}_{90}=M_1 \\cdot T^0_{90}; \\; T^{1.2}_{90}=M_2 \\cdot T^0_{90}; \\; T^{1.3}_{90}=M_3 \\cdot T^0_{90}}").

These ![(20, 21, 29); (5, 12, 13); (8, 15,
17)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%2820%2C+21%2C+29%29%3B+%285%2C+12%2C+13%29%3B+%288%2C+15%2C+17%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(20, 21, 29); (5, 12, 13); (8, 15, 17)") represent the next level in
the hierarchy of primitive triples. From those 3 we get a further
generations of 9 and then 27 and so on by multiplying each triple of the
previous generation by each of the transformation matrices. What serial
multiplication by
![\\mathbf{M\_1}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathbf%7BM_1%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\mathbf{M_1}") does is to create right triangles approaching the
half-square or
![\\tfrac{\\pi}{4}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B%5Cpi%7D%7B4%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{\\pi}{4}") or
![45^{\\circ}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=45%5E%7B%5Ccirc%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"45^{\\circ}") triangle. Thus, in the lineage of multiplication by
![M\_1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"M_1") we get triangles such that

![\\textrm{AM}\\left( \\dfrac{c}{a}, \\dfrac{c}{b} \\right)
\\to\\sqrt{2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctextrm%7BAM%7D%5Cleft%28+%5Cdfrac%7Bc%7D%7Ba%7D%2C+%5Cdfrac%7Bc%7D%7Bb%7D+%5Cright%29+%5Cto%5Csqrt%7B2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\textrm{AM}\\left( \\dfrac{c}{a}, \\dfrac{c}{b} \\right) \\to\\sqrt{2}")

For example, ![(119, 120,
169)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28119%2C+120%2C+169%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(119, 120, 169)") the 2nd generation triple in the
![\\mathbf{M\_1}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathbf%7BM_1%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\mathbf{M_1}") lineage yields ![\\textrm{AM}\\left( \\tfrac{169}{119},
\\tfrac{169}{120} \\right) =
1.414251](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctextrm%7BAM%7D%5Cleft%28+%5Ctfrac%7B169%7D%7B119%7D%2C+%5Ctfrac%7B169%7D%7B120%7D+%5Cright%29+%3D+1.414251&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\textrm{AM}\\left( \\tfrac{169}{119}, \\tfrac{169}{120} \\right) = 1.414251"),
which approximates
![\\sqrt{2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csqrt%7B2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\sqrt{2}") correctly to 4 decimal places. In contrast, the matrices
![\\mathbf{M\_2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathbf%7BM_2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\mathbf{M_2}") and
![\\mathbf{M\_3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathbf%7BM_3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\mathbf{M_3}") drive the triangles towards the state where one of the
sides and the hypotenuse are nearly equal. When we plot the rational
points generated by the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples on the unit circle we get a fractal structure covering
the primary octant of the said circle (Figure 3).  
[![triples\_bbk\_on\_circ](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/12/triples_bbk_on_circ.png?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/12/triples_bbk_on_circ.png)Figure
3. Rational points on the unit circle derived from
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples with those derived from the primordial triple ![(3, 4,
5)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%283%2C+4%2C+5%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(3, 4, 5)") and 3 subsequent generations of triples indicated by disks
of decreasing size.

This structure is characterized by “zones of exclusion” defined by the
high-ranked triples. The biggest such zone is defined by the primordial
![(3, 4,
5)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%283%2C+4%2C+5%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(3, 4, 5)") triple, followed by those of generation-1 and so on (Figure
3). However, if one zooms in on the smaller zones of exclusion one sees
a fractal structure mirroring the larger zones.

If we order the triples by the ascending value of
![c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "c")
then we get a remarkable relationship for the count of triples with
![c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "c")
below or equal to a certain number
![N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "N"):

![\\dfrac{\\\#(a, b, c \\le N)}{N} \\simeq
\\dfrac{1}{2\\pi}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdfrac%7B%5C%23%28a%2C+b%2C+c+%5Cle+N%29%7D%7BN%7D+%5Csimeq+%5Cdfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%5Cpi%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\dfrac{\\#(a, b, c \\le N)}{N} \\simeq \\dfrac{1}{2\\pi}")

This famous result of the senior Derrick Lehmer, which can be easily
numerically visualized (Figure 4), has links to Leonhard Euler’s
discovery of the
![\\zeta](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Czeta&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\zeta") function.

Its formal proof is complicated (for the mathematical layman) and yields
an additional logarithmic term. However, the basic concept leading to to
Lehmer’s asymptotic result is readily understood. Euler had famously
shown that the probability of two randomly chosen positive integers ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") having
![\\textrm{GCD}(u,v)=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctextrm%7BGCD%7D%28u%2Cv%29%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\textrm{GCD}(u,v)=1"), i.e. being mutually prime, is
![\\tfrac{1}{\\zeta(2)}=\\tfrac{6}{\\pi^2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B%5Czeta%282%29%7D%3D%5Ctfrac%7B6%7D%7B%5Cpi%5E2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{1}{\\zeta(2)}=\\tfrac{6}{\\pi^2}"). In the above formula for
obtaining
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples we start with two mutually prime integers ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v"). Thus, the probability of getting two such mutually prime
integers is defined by this formula of Euler. The hypotenuse
![c=u^2+v^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c%3Du%5E2%2Bv%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"c=u^2+v^2"); hence, we can see the points defined by ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") for all ![c \\le
N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c+%5Cle+N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"c \\le N") lying inside or on a circle with radius
![\\sqrt{N}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csqrt%7BN%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\sqrt{N}") and area ![\\pi
N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cpi+N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\pi N"). However, we do not consider the entire circle; as we saw
above, because of the ordering and sign considerations we only account
for one octant of a circle, i.e.
![\\tfrac{1}{8}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B8%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{1}{8}") of its total area. Hence, we get the number of points
defined by the mutually prime ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") such that ![u^2+v^2 \\le
N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%5E2%2Bv%5E2+%5Cle+N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u^2+v^2 \\le N") to be ![\\tfrac{6}{\\pi^2} \\cdot \\pi N \\cdot
\\tfrac{1}{8} = \\tfrac{3}{4
\\pi}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B6%7D%7B%5Cpi%5E2%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cpi+N+%5Ccdot+%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B8%7D+%3D+%5Ctfrac%7B3%7D%7B4+%5Cpi%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{6}{\\pi^2} \\cdot \\pi N \\cdot \\tfrac{1}{8} = \\tfrac{3}{4 \\pi}").
However, not all these points correspond to valid triples. As we saw
above, we have to exclude triples emerging from mutually prime ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") which are both odd. This approximately eliminates
![\\tfrac{1}{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{1}{3}") of the possible mutually prime couplings (e.g. for 1,
2, 3, we have 1, 2 and 2, 3 as valid couplings but 1,3 is not).
Therefore, we have multiply the value ![\\tfrac{3}{4
\\pi}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B3%7D%7B4+%5Cpi%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{3}{4 \\pi}") by a correction factor of
![\\tfrac{2}{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B2%7D%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{2}{3}"), leading to the above asymptotic formula of Lehmer.

**Divisibility and composition of triples**  
The triples illustrate a deep link between geometry and arithmetic in
terms of their divisibility and composition. Some of these facts have
probably been known since antiquity for the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples. One trivial feature is that all the odd numbers will
be represented among the shortest sides for both the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") and
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples. Since adjacent odd and even numbers are coprime, and
thus valid ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") for generating the shortest side
![a](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "a"),
they give rise to the odd numbers because the difference of the squares
of two adjacent numbers is an odd number
![2n+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=2n%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"2n+1"). Less trivially, in the case of the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples we also get the multiples of 4 starting from 8 onward
as the shortest side
![a](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "a").
This happens when we have even numbers couple with 1 (being naturally
coprime), such as ![u=4,
v=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D4%2C+v%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=4, v=1"). Here
![b=u^2-v^2=15](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=b%3Du%5E2-v%5E2%3D15&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"b=u^2-v^2=15") and
![a=2uv=8](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a%3D2uv%3D8&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a=2uv=8"); similarly, ![u=6,
v=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D6%2C+v%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=6, v=1") would yield ![b=35,
a=12](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=b%3D35%2C+a%3D12&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"b=35, a=12") and so on. In the case of the
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples we similarly have in addition to the odd numbers, all
multiples of 8 from 16 onward. These come from consecutive odd numbers,
which being coprime can be valid ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") to generate
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples. Thus, ![u=2n+1,
v=2n+3](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D2n%2B1%2C+v%3D2n%2B3&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=2n+1, v=2n+3") yields
![a=8(n+1)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a%3D8%28n%2B1%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a=8(n+1)"), which from the
![n=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=1") onward will yield 16, 24…

A further relationship is related to the hypotenuse
![c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "c") of
a primitive
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triple: it is always an integer of the form
![4n+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=4n%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"4n+1"). This can be understood thusly: Given ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") as the coprime generators of a primitive triple let them be
respectively odd and even coprime numbers. We can write
![u=2k+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D2k%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=2k+1") and
![v=2j](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=v%3D2j&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"v=2j"). ![\\therefore c=u^2+v^2 = 4k^2+ 4k+ 1+ 4j^2=
4(k^2+k+j^2)+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctherefore+c%3Du%5E2%2Bv%5E2+%3D+4k%5E2%2B+4k%2B+1%2B+4j%5E2%3D+4%28k%5E2%2Bk%2Bj%5E2%29%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\therefore c=u^2+v^2 = 4k^2+ 4k+ 1+ 4j^2= 4(k^2+k+j^2)+1"). Thus, the
hypotenuse will be of the form
![4n+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=4n%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"4n+1") where ![n=1, 2,
3..](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D1%2C+2%2C+3..&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=1, 2, 3.."). A similar relationship exists for
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples where every longest side
![c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "c") is
always of the form ![6n+1; n=1,2,
3...](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=6n%2B1%3B+n%3D1%2C2%2C+3...&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"6n+1; n=1,2, 3..."). This is harder to demonstrate generally than for
the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") example. But we can do it informally by breaking this down to
multiple cases. First, consider a coprime ![u,
v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2C+v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u, v") which are successive odd and even numbers. We can write them as
![v=2k+1,
u=2k+2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=v%3D2k%2B1%2C+u%3D2k%2B2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"v=2k+1, u=2k+2"). We then have ![c=u^2+uv+v^2=
6(2k^2+3k+1)+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c%3Du%5E2%2Buv%2Bv%5E2%3D+6%282k%5E2%2B3k%2B1%29%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"c=u^2+uv+v^2= 6(2k^2+3k+1)+1") which is an integer of the form
![6n+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=6n%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"6n+1"). Second, if we a consecutive odd coprime numbers, e.g. 1, 3 they
will be of the form ![u=2k+3,
v=2k+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D2k%2B3%2C+v%3D2k%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=2k+3, v=2k+1") then ![c=6(2k^2 + 4k +
2)+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c%3D6%282k%5E2+%2B+4k+%2B+2%29%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"c=6(2k^2 + 4k + 2)+1"), which is an integer of the form
![6n+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=6n%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"6n+1"). Now, if we instead consider a coprime pair of the form
![u=2k+4,
v=2k+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D2k%2B4%2C+v%3D2k%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=2k+4, v=2k+1") then ![c=u^2+uv+v^2= 3(4k^2 + 10k +
7)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c%3Du%5E2%2Buv%2Bv%5E2%3D+3%284k%5E2+%2B+10k+%2B+7%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"c=u^2+uv+v^2= 3(4k^2 + 10k + 7)"). This number will not be of the form
![6n+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=6n%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"6n+1") but will be eliminated from the list because it is not a
primitive triple being divisible by 3. For example, as we saw above
while applying the correction factor for the counts, ![u=4,
v=1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D4%2C+v%3D1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=4, v=1") or ![u=8,
v=3](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%3D8%2C+v%3D3&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u=8, v=3") are such eliminated pairs. By extending this argument we can
see that the only
![c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "c")
that remain will be of the form
![6n+1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=6n%2B1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"6n+1") because the other coprime
![u,v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=u%2Cv&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"u,v") pairs will generate non-primitive triples.

For the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples there is another interesting pattern: The most
primitive triple is ![3, 4,
5](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=3%2C+4%2C+5&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"3, 4, 5"). At least 1 of the 3 sides of every subsequent primitive
triple will be divisible by 3, 4 and 5. For example: sorted by the
hypotenuse
![c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "c")
the 29th triple ![T\_{90}^{29}= 57, 176,
185](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D%5E%7B29%7D%3D+57%2C+176%2C+185&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}^{29}= 57, 176, 185") has the 1st side divisible by 3, the 2nd by
4 and the 3rd by 5. The 30th triple ![T\_{90}^{30}= 104, 153,
185](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D%5E%7B30%7D%3D+104%2C+153%2C+185&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}^{30}= 104, 153, 185") has the 1st side divisible by 4, the 2nd
by 3, and the 3rd by 5. In other cases, 2 of these might divide the same
side: e.g. ![T\_{90}^{2}= 5, 12,
13](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D%5E%7B2%7D%3D+5%2C+12%2C+13&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}^{2}= 5, 12, 13"), where 5 divides the 1st side and 3, 4 divide
the second. Sometimes all 3 numbers might divide the same side, e.g.
![T\_{90}^{9}= 11, 60,
61](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D%5E%7B9%7D%3D+11%2C+60%2C+61&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}^{9}= 11, 60, 61") where the 2nd side is divisible by 3, 4, 5. A
comparable relationship exists for
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples: at least 1 of the 3 sides of the triple will be
divided by at least 2 of the following numbers: 3, 5, 7, 8. Of these 3,
5, and 8 divide at least 1 side of around
![\\tfrac{2}{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B2%7D%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{2}{3}") of the
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples and 7 divides at least one side of around
![\\tfrac{3}{4}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7B3%7D%7B4%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{3}{4}") of the
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples.

Finally, in passing, one may also note that it has been known for a
while that the terms meru-średhi (known in the occident as the Fibonacci
sequence) can be used to generate particular
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triples, though not all of them are primitive.  
Let ![f=1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8,
13...](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f%3D1%2C+1%2C+2%2C+3%2C+5%2C+8%2C+13...&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"f=1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13...") be the średhi, then a
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triple is defined by:  
![x = f\[n\] \\cdot f\[n+3\]\\\\ y = 2f\[n+1\] \\cdot f\[n+2\]\\\\ z =
f\[2n+3\]](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x+%3D+f%5Bn%5D+%5Ccdot+f%5Bn%2B3%5D%5C%5C+y+%3D+2f%5Bn%2B1%5D+%5Ccdot+f%5Bn%2B2%5D%5C%5C+z+%3D+f%5B2n%2B3%5D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x = f[n] \\cdot f[n+3]\\\\ y = 2f[n+1] \\cdot f[n+2]\\\\ z = f[2n+3]")  
For example, if we take
![n=5](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D5&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=5"), we get the
![T\_{90}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B90%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{90}") triple: 105, 208, 233

Inspired this, we can derive a similar formula of the
![T\_{120}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=T_%7B120%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"T_{120}") triples (again all will not be primitive):
