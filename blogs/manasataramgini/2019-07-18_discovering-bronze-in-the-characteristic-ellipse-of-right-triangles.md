**The arithmetic mean square of a right triangle**  
An entire family of right triangles that includes all the different
forms of right triangles defined in terms of the proportion of their
legs can be obtained by setting their altitude to a constant
![a](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "a")
and letting their base
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "x")
vary. The right-angle vertex of these triangles, i.e. that which
contains the right angle is common to both legs and constant across the
family. By the above definition the altitude vertex, i.e. that defined
by the terminus of the altitude, is also constant. The base vertex, i.e.
that defined by the terminus of the base can be seen as continuously
varying as it moves along the line perpendicular to the altitude.

[![arithmetic\_mean\_sqr\_righttriangle\_Fig1](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/arithmetic_mean_sqr_righttriangle_fig1.png?w=568&h=566)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/arithmetic_mean_sqr_righttriangle_fig1.png)Figure
1

Consider Figure 1:  
For a given right triangle (
![\\triangle{ABC}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctriangle%7BABC%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\triangle{ABC}")) the half-hypotenuse square is defined as the square
erected on half of its hypotenuse (
![\\square{BDEF}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csquare%7BBDEF%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\square{BDEF}")). Then we have the following:

● The feet of the perpendiculars dropped from the vertex of the
half-hypotenuse
![(E)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28E%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(E)") square diagonally opposite to the base vertex to the base and the
altitude of the given right triangle, together with its right-angle
vertex defines a square
![(\\square{CMEN})](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28%5Csquare%7BCMEN%7D%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(\\square{CMEN})").

● The area of this square is the sum of the areas of the given right
triangle and the half-hypotenuse square.

● The side of this square is the arithmetic mean of the two legs
![(\\overline{CA}=a,
\\overline{CB}=x)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28%5Coverline%7BCA%7D%3Da%2C+%5Coverline%7BCB%7D%3Dx%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(\\overline{CA}=a, \\overline{CB}=x)") of the given right triangle.

Hence, the above-defined square
![(\\square{CMEN})](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28%5Csquare%7BCMEN%7D%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(\\square{CMEN})") is termed the arithmetic mean square of a give right
triangle.

By examining Figure 1, registering all the self-evident congruences and
performing the dissections indicated by the colored and marked areas it
is possible to obtain “wordless” proofs of statements 1 and 2
![\_{...\\blacksquare}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=_%7B...%5Cblacksquare%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"_{...\\blacksquare}")

By bhujā-koṭi-karṇa-nyāya the area of the half-hypotenuse square is
![\\tfrac{a^2+x^2}{4}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Ba%5E2%2Bx%5E2%7D%7B4%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{a^2+x^2}{4}"). Thus,

![A(\\square{CMEN})=A(\\square{BDEF}) + A(\\triangle{ABC}) =
\\tfrac{a^2+x^2}{4}
+\\tfrac{ax}{2}=\\left(\\tfrac{a+x}{2}\\right)^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=A%28%5Csquare%7BCMEN%7D%29%3DA%28%5Csquare%7BBDEF%7D%29+%2B+A%28%5Ctriangle%7BABC%7D%29+%3D+%5Ctfrac%7Ba%5E2%2Bx%5E2%7D%7B4%7D+%2B%5Ctfrac%7Bax%7D%7B2%7D%3D%5Cleft%28%5Ctfrac%7Ba%2Bx%7D%7B2%7D%5Cright%29%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"A(\\square{CMEN})=A(\\square{BDEF}) + A(\\triangle{ABC}) = \\tfrac{a^2+x^2}{4} +\\tfrac{ax}{2}=\\left(\\tfrac{a+x}{2}\\right)^2")

Thus, the side of
![\\square{CMEN}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csquare%7BCMEN%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\square{CMEN}") is the arithmetic mean of the legs of the given right
triangle
![A(\\triangle{ABC})](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=A%28%5Ctriangle%7BABC%7D%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"A(\\triangle{ABC})")
![\_{...\\blacksquare}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=_%7B...%5Cblacksquare%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"_{...\\blacksquare}"). Thus we term this square the arithmetic mean
square.

**The characteristic ellipse of right triangles**  
We also note that the shared vertex of the half-hypotenuse square and
the arithmetic mean square of a right triangle (point
![E](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=E&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "E"))
is the vertex of the semicircle erected on the hypotenuse of the right
triangle (Figure 1). Thus, the segment
![\\overline{EF}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Coverline%7BEF%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\overline{EF}") will be the diameter of the maximal circle that can be
inscribed in the above semicircle (Figure 2). Now, if we draw a
perpendicular to the base of the right triangle at the base vertex
![B](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=B&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "B")
then the perpendicular will cut the inscribed maximal circle at two
points ![G,
H](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G%2C+H&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"G, H") (Figure 2). What will be the locus of ![G,
H](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G%2C+H&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"G, H") as the base of the triangle
![\\overline{CB}=x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Coverline%7BCB%7D%3Dx&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\overline{CB}=x") grows in size? In what range of
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "x") in
terms of the altitude
![a](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "a")
will this intersection will happen (i.e. for what range of
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "x") in
![a](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "a")
units will the locus exist)?

[![triangle\_ellipse\_problem\_Fig2](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/triangle_ellipse_problem_fig2.png?w=589&h=550)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/triangle_ellipse_problem_fig2.png)

Figure 2

To answer the above let us place point
![C](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=C&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "C") at
origin. Then, given the above-described arithmetic mean square of a
right triangle we have the coordinates of its vertex and that of the
semicircle erected on the hypotenuse to be
![E=(\\tfrac{a+x}{2},\\tfrac{a+x}{2})](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=E%3D%28%5Ctfrac%7Ba%2Bx%7D%7B2%7D%2C%5Ctfrac%7Ba%2Bx%7D%7B2%7D%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"E=(\\tfrac{a+x}{2},\\tfrac{a+x}{2})"). This is also one end of a
diameter of the maximal circle inscribed in the semicircle. The second
end of this diameter is the midpoint of the hypotenuse
![F](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=F&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "F").
Therefore, the coordinates of ![F=(\\tfrac{x}{2},
\\tfrac{a}{2})](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=F%3D%28%5Ctfrac%7Bx%7D%7B2%7D%2C+%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B2%7D%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"F=(\\tfrac{x}{2}, \\tfrac{a}{2})"). Thus, we can get the midpoint of
the the segment
![\\overline{EF}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Coverline%7BEF%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\overline{EF}") to be
![I=(\\tfrac{2x+a}{4},\\tfrac{x+2a}{4})](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=I%3D%28%5Ctfrac%7B2x%2Ba%7D%7B4%7D%2C%5Ctfrac%7Bx%2B2a%7D%7B4%7D%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"I=(\\tfrac{2x+a}{4},\\tfrac{x+2a}{4})"). The coordinates of point
![G](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "G")
(or of point
![F](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=F&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "F")),
which lies on the desired locus will be
![G=(x,y)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G%3D%28x%2Cy%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"G=(x,y)"). From circle ![\\circ
I](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ccirc+I&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\circ I") using the equivalence of its two radii we can write the
following:  
![\\overline{FI}^2=\\overline{GI}^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Coverline%7BFI%7D%5E2%3D%5Coverline%7BGI%7D%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\overline{FI}^2=\\overline{GI}^2")

But
![\\overline{FI}^2=\\tfrac{1}{4}\\overline{AB}^2=\\tfrac{x^2+a^2}{4}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Coverline%7BFI%7D%5E2%3D%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B4%7D%5Coverline%7BAB%7D%5E2%3D%5Ctfrac%7Bx%5E2%2Ba%5E2%7D%7B4%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\overline{FI}^2=\\tfrac{1}{4}\\overline{AB}^2=\\tfrac{x^2+a^2}{4}")

![\\therefore
(\\tfrac{a+2x}{4}-x)^2+(\\tfrac{x+2a}{4}-y)^2=\\tfrac{x^2+a^2}{4}\\\\\[7pt\]
(\\tfrac{a+2x-4x}{4})^2+(\\tfrac{x+2a-4y}{4})^2=\\tfrac{x^2+a^2}{4}\\\\\[7pt\]
(a-2x)^2+(x+2a-4y)^2=x^2+a^2\\\\\[7pt\]
x^2-2xy+4y^2-4ay+a^2=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctherefore+%28%5Ctfrac%7Ba%2B2x%7D%7B4%7D-x%29%5E2%2B%28%5Ctfrac%7Bx%2B2a%7D%7B4%7D-y%29%5E2%3D%5Ctfrac%7Bx%5E2%2Ba%5E2%7D%7B4%7D%5C%5C%5B7pt%5D+%28%5Ctfrac%7Ba%2B2x-4x%7D%7B4%7D%29%5E2%2B%28%5Ctfrac%7Bx%2B2a-4y%7D%7B4%7D%29%5E2%3D%5Ctfrac%7Bx%5E2%2Ba%5E2%7D%7B4%7D%5C%5C%5B7pt%5D+%28a-2x%29%5E2%2B%28x%2B2a-4y%29%5E2%3Dx%5E2%2Ba%5E2%5C%5C%5B7pt%5D+x%5E2-2xy%2B4y%5E2-4ay%2Ba%5E2%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\therefore (\\tfrac{a+2x}{4}-x)^2+(\\tfrac{x+2a}{4}-y)^2=\\tfrac{x^2+a^2}{4}\\\\[7pt] (\\tfrac{a+2x-4x}{4})^2+(\\tfrac{x+2a-4y}{4})^2=\\tfrac{x^2+a^2}{4}\\\\[7pt] (a-2x)^2+(x+2a-4y)^2=x^2+a^2\\\\[7pt] x^2-2xy+4y^2-4ay+a^2=0")

The above equation of the locus is a quadratic equation in two
variables. Hence, it is a conic. Now given a general conic
![Ax^2+Bxy+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=Ax%5E2%2BBxy%2BCy%5E2%2BDx%2BEy%2BF%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"Ax^2+Bxy+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0"), its shape is determined by the conic
discriminant:

![\\delta\_c=-4\\Delta=-4 \\begin{vmatrix} A & B \\big /2\\\\ B \\big
/2& C \\end{vmatrix}
=B^2-4AC](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdelta_c%3D-4%5CDelta%3D-4+%5Cbegin%7Bvmatrix%7D+A+%26+B+%5Cbig+%2F2%5C%5C+B+%5Cbig+%2F2%26+C+%5Cend%7Bvmatrix%7D+%3DB%5E2-4AC&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\delta_c=-4\\Delta=-4 \\begin{vmatrix} A & B \\big /2\\\\ B \\big /2& C \\end{vmatrix} =B^2-4AC")

If
![\\delta\_c\<0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdelta_c%3C0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\delta_c\<0") then we have an ellipse. In the special case of ![A=C,
B=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=A%3DC%2C+B%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"A=C, B=0") it reduces to a circle. If
![\\delta\_c=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdelta_c%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\delta_c=0") we have a parabola. If
![\\delta\_c\>0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdelta_c%3E0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\delta_c\>0") we have a hyperbola. In our case ![\\delta\_c=4-4
\\times 1 \\times
4=-12](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdelta_c%3D4-4+%5Ctimes+1+%5Ctimes+4%3D-12&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\delta_c=4-4 \\times 1 \\times 4=-12"). Thus, the desired locus is an
ellipse (Figure 3).

[![triangle\_ellipse\_problem\_Fig3](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/triangle_ellipse_problem_fig3.png?w=617&h=525)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/triangle_ellipse_problem_fig3.png)

Figure 3

We can now rewrite the equation of the ellipse explicitly as:  
![\\dfrac{x+2a \\pm
\\sqrt{4ax-3x^2}}{4}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdfrac%7Bx%2B2a+%5Cpm+%5Csqrt%7B4ax-3x%5E2%7D%7D%7B4%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\dfrac{x+2a \\pm \\sqrt{4ax-3x^2}}{4}")

The discriminant of this equation is:  
![\\Delta=\\sqrt{4ax-3x^2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5CDelta%3D%5Csqrt%7B4ax-3x%5E2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\Delta=\\sqrt{4ax-3x^2}"); it will be real only when: ![0 \\le x \\le
\\tfrac{4a}{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=0+%5Cle+x+%5Cle+%5Ctfrac%7B4a%7D%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"0 \\le x \\le \\tfrac{4a}{3}")

From this we can see that the ellipse will exist only in the interval:  
![\\left(0,\\tfrac{a}{2}\\right)..\\left(\\tfrac{4a}{3},
\\tfrac{5a}{6}\\right)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cleft%280%2C%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B2%7D%5Cright%29..%5Cleft%28%5Ctfrac%7B4a%7D%7B3%7D%2C+%5Ctfrac%7B5a%7D%7B6%7D%5Cright%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\left(0,\\tfrac{a}{2}\\right)..\\left(\\tfrac{4a}{3}, \\tfrac{5a}{6}\\right)")

Thus, at those two points ![G,
H](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G%2C+H&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"G, H") become coincident. Thus, the altitude of the right triangles is
a tangent to this ellipse at
![\\left(0,\\tfrac{a}{2}\\right)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cleft%280%2C%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B2%7D%5Cright%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\left(0,\\tfrac{a}{2}\\right)") (Figure 3). Further, since we have the
coordinates of point
![I](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=I&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "I"),
the center of the maximal circle inscribed in the hypotenuse semicircle,
we can show that as
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "x")
increases
![I](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=I&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "I")
moves along the line
![y=\\tfrac{1}{2}x+\\tfrac{3a}{8}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y%3D%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7Dx%2B%5Ctfrac%7B3a%7D%7B8%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y=\\tfrac{1}{2}x+\\tfrac{3a}{8}"). Thus, outside the above interval we
see that the right triangle’s base moves beyond the maximal inscribed
circle and the perpendicular to the base at
![B](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=B&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "B")
will never touch this circle.

A shape of a triangle is characterized by the relative proportion of its
3 sides. For a right triangle we have further constraint; hence its
shape is defined by the relative proportion of its legs. Thus, a right
triangle with legs in proportion
![a\_1:b\_1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a_1%3Ab_1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a_1:b_1") is the same as that with
![b\_1:a\_1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=b_1%3Aa_1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"b_1:a_1") — just that they are rotated with respect to each other.
Using this concept we, can see that various landmarks of the
characteristic ellipse correspond to notable underlying right triangles
(Figure 4). The extreme left bound of the ellipse corresponds to the
degenerate case where one leg is 0 and the hypotenuse and the other leg
coincide. As we determined above, the right bound of the ellipse
corresponds to
![x=\\tfrac{4a}{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%3D%5Ctfrac%7B4a%7D%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x=\\tfrac{4a}{3}"). Thus, this corresponds to the famous
![3,4,5](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=3%2C4%2C5&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"3,4,5") triangle. It is remarkable how
![3,4,5](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=3%2C4%2C5&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"3,4,5") triangle, which represents the most basic bhujā-koṭi-karṇa
triplet, comes up as a natural feature associated with the
characteristic ellipse.

[![special\_triangles\_Fig4](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/special_triangles_fig4.png?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/special_triangles_fig4.png)Figure
4

Now that we have its equation we can also see that the ellipse will have
a maximum at the point
![(a,a)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28a%2Ca%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(a,a)") and a minimum at the point ![\\left(\\tfrac{a}{3},
\\tfrac{a}{3}\\right)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cleft%28%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B3%7D%2C+%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B3%7D%5Cright%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\left(\\tfrac{a}{3}, \\tfrac{a}{3}\\right)"). The maximum corresponds
to isosceles right triangle (Figure 4). The minimum of the ellipse
corresponds the
![1,3,\\sqrt{10}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=1%2C3%2C%5Csqrt%7B10%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"1,3,\\sqrt{10}") triangle (Figure 4). At both these positions the
separation between the ellipse-generating points ![G,
H](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G%2C+H&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"G, H") is
![\\tfrac{a}{2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{a}{2}").

When
![x=\\tfrac{2a}{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%3D%5Ctfrac%7B2a%7D%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x=\\tfrac{2a}{3}"), the ellipse-generating line passes through the
center of the ellipse, point ![C\_e=\\left(\\tfrac{2a}{3},
\\tfrac{2a}{3}\\right)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=C_e%3D%5Cleft%28%5Ctfrac%7B2a%7D%7B3%7D%2C+%5Ctfrac%7B2a%7D%7B3%7D%5Cright%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"C_e=\\left(\\tfrac{2a}{3}, \\tfrac{2a}{3}\\right)") (Figure 4). This
results in the maximal separation attained between the
ellipse-generating points ![G,
H](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G%2C+H&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"G, H") of
![\\tfrac{a}{\\sqrt{3}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B%5Csqrt%7B3%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{a}{\\sqrt{3}}"). This corresponds to the
![2,3,\\sqrt{13}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=2%2C3%2C%5Csqrt%7B13%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"2,3,\\sqrt{13}") triangle. In this triangle the ratio of the sum of the
long and middle sides to the short side is the Bronze ratio, which we
shall encounter again discuss in greater detail below.

When
![x=\\tfrac{a}{2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%3D%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x=\\tfrac{a}{2}") we have the underlying
![1,2,\\sqrt{5}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=1%2C2%2C%5Csqrt%7B5%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"1,2,\\sqrt{5}") triangle (Figure 4). As is obvious, the ratio of the
sum of the short and the long side of this triangle to the middle side
is the Golden ratio
![(\\phi)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28%5Cphi%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"(\\phi)"). Thus, not surprisingly, the separation between the
ellipse-generating points at this value of
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "x") is

only in this configuration the lines drawn parallel to the base of the

![G,H](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G%2CH&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0

Between ![\\tfrac{3a}{4} \\le x \\le

"\\tfrac{3a}{4} \\le x \\le \\tfrac{4a}{3}") (colored section of ellipse

"x=a"). However, to the left of that region every triangle has a unique
shape.

**The bronze ratio and the characteristic ellipse**  
Given the general conic equation
![Ax^2+Bxy+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=Ax%5E2%2BBxy%2BCy%5E2%2BDx%2BEy%2BF%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"Ax^2+Bxy+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0") one can calculate the eccentricity of the
corresponding ellipse as:  
![e\_e=\\sqrt{\\dfrac{2\\sqrt{(A-C)^2+B^2}}{A+C+\\sqrt{(A-C)^2+B^2}}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=e_e%3D%5Csqrt%7B%5Cdfrac%7B2%5Csqrt%7B%28A-C%29%5E2%2BB%5E2%7D%7D%7BA%2BC%2B%5Csqrt%7B%28A-C%29%5E2%2BB%5E2%7D%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"e_e=\\sqrt{\\dfrac{2\\sqrt{(A-C)^2+B^2}}{A+C+\\sqrt{(A-C)^2+B^2}}}")

By substituting the requisite values, we get  
![e\_e=\\sqrt{\\dfrac{5\\sqrt{13}-13}{6}} \\approx
0.9154012](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=e_e%3D%5Csqrt%7B%5Cdfrac%7B5%5Csqrt%7B13%7D-13%7D%7B6%7D%7D+%5Capprox+0.9154012&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"e_e=\\sqrt{\\dfrac{5\\sqrt{13}-13}{6}} \\approx 0.9154012")

Now, we consider the Bronze ratio ![\\beta=\\tfrac{3+\\sqrt{13}}{2}
\\approx
3.302776](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cbeta%3D%5Ctfrac%7B3%2B%5Csqrt%7B13%7D%7D%7B2%7D+%5Capprox+3.302776&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\beta=\\tfrac{3+\\sqrt{13}}{2} \\approx 3.302776"). We observe that
![\\beta'=\\tfrac{1}{\\beta}=\\beta-3 \\approx
0.302776](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cbeta%27%3D%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B%5Cbeta%7D%3D%5Cbeta-3+%5Capprox+0.302776&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\beta'=\\tfrac{1}{\\beta}=\\beta-3 \\approx 0.302776"). Thus,
![\\beta,
-\\beta'](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cbeta%2C+-%5Cbeta%27&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\beta, -\\beta'") are conjugate roots of the quadratic equation
![x^2-3x-1=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%5E2-3x-1%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x^2-3x-1=0"). One immediately notes the parallel to the Golden ratio
![\\phi](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cphi&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\phi"): ![\\phi'
=\\tfrac{1}{\\phi}=\\phi-1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cphi%27+%3D%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B%5Cphi%7D%3D%5Cphi-1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\phi' =\\tfrac{1}{\\phi}=\\phi-1") and likewise ![\\phi,
-\\phi'](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cphi%2C+-%5Cphi%27&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\phi, -\\phi'") are the roots of the equation
![x^2-x-1=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%5E2-x-1%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x^2-x-1=0") (see appendix for more). Thus, we can write the
eccentricity of the characteristic ellipse as:  
![\\sqrt{\\dfrac{5\\beta'+1}{3}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csqrt%7B%5Cdfrac%7B5%5Cbeta%27%2B1%7D%7B3%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\sqrt{\\dfrac{5\\beta'+1}{3}}")

With the eccentricity we can determine the axes of the ellipse; we see
that:  
![a\_{smin}=\\dfrac{a\\sqrt{1+\\beta}}{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a_%7Bsmin%7D%3D%5Cdfrac%7Ba%5Csqrt%7B1%2B%5Cbeta%7D%7D%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a_{smin}=\\dfrac{a\\sqrt{1+\\beta}}{3}") …semi-major axis

![a\_{smaj}=\\dfrac{a}{\\sqrt{3(1+\\beta)}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a_%7Bsmaj%7D%3D%5Cdfrac%7Ba%7D%7B%5Csqrt%7B3%281%2B%5Cbeta%29%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"a_{smaj}=\\dfrac{a}{\\sqrt{3(1+\\beta)}}") …semi-minor axis

As an aside the area of the characteristic ellipse is therefore
![A=\\tfrac{\\pi
a^2}{3\\sqrt{3}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=A%3D%5Ctfrac%7B%5Cpi+a%5E2%7D%7B3%5Csqrt%7B3%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"A=\\tfrac{\\pi a^2}{3\\sqrt{3}}"). We also get the aspect ratio of the
ellipse to be:  
![AR=\\dfrac{a\_{smaj}}{a\_{smin}}=\\dfrac{1+\\beta}{\\sqrt{3}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=AR%3D%5Cdfrac%7Ba_%7Bsmaj%7D%7D%7Ba_%7Bsmin%7D%7D%3D%5Cdfrac%7B1%2B%5Cbeta%7D%7B%5Csqrt%7B3%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"AR=\\dfrac{a_{smaj}}{a_{smin}}=\\dfrac{1+\\beta}{\\sqrt{3}}")

Interestingly, the connection of the characteristic ellipse to the
bronze ratio does not end with the parameters of the ellipse itself. Its
inclination, i.e. the slope of the line joining the two foci of the
ellipse (i.e. the tangent of the angle made by this focal line with the
base of the right triangles) is
![\\beta'](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cbeta%27&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\beta'"). Thus, the equation of the local line of the characteristic
ellipse is:  
![y=\\beta'x+\\dfrac{2a}{1+\\beta}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y%3D%5Cbeta%27x%2B%5Cdfrac%7B2a%7D%7B1%2B%5Cbeta%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y=\\beta'x+\\dfrac{2a}{1+\\beta}")

[![triangle\_ellipse\_problem\_Fig5](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/triangle_ellipse_problem_fig5.png?w=578&h=443)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/triangle_ellipse_problem_fig5.png)Figure
5

Interestingly, when
![x=\\beta'](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x%3D%5Cbeta%27&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x=\\beta'"), it is only time the diameter of ellipse-generating circle
perpendicular to the hypotenuse of the right triangle (i.e.
![\\overline{EF}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Coverline%7BEF%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\overline{EF}")) becomes parallel to the focal line of the ellipse and
acquires the equation:  
![y=\\beta'(x+\\dfrac{3a}{2})](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y%3D%5Cbeta%27%28x%2B%5Cdfrac%7B3a%7D%7B2%7D%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y=\\beta'(x+\\dfrac{3a}{2})") (Figure 5).

Thus, by doing some algebra we can see that the difference in the
intercepts is
![\\tfrac{a}{26\\beta+8}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Ba%7D%7B26%5Cbeta%2B8%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\tfrac{a}{26\\beta+8}")

This is one of few rare occurrences of the Bronze ratio as persistent
feature in geometry and to our knowledge is described here for the first
time.

**Appendix: Bronze ratio**  
A simple geometric interpretation of the Golden ratio
![\\phi](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cphi&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\phi") goes thus: If we construct a rectangle such that upon removing
a square whose side is equal to the smaller side of the said rectangle
we are left with a remainder rectangle whose aspect ratio is the same as
the original rectangle then the aspect ratio of these rectangles is
![\\phi](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cphi&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\phi") (Figure 6). Likewise, if we get a similarly-shaped remainder
rectangle upon removing two equal squares whose sides are equal to the
shorter side of the starting rectangle then their aspect ratio is the
Silver ratio
![\\sigma=1+\\sqrt{2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csigma%3D1%2B%5Csqrt%7B2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\sigma=1+\\sqrt{2}") (Figure 6). These two ratios are also associated
with diagonals of a pentagon and an octagon respectively. If upon
removing 3 squares we have similarly proportioned remainder rectangle as
the original one then their aspect ratio is the Bronze ratio
![\\beta](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cbeta&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"\\beta"). This is not associated with any polygon.

[![gold\_Silv\_bron\_Fig6](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/gold_silv_bron_fig6.png?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/gold_silv_bron_fig6.png)Figure
6

All these “metallic” ratios, their reciprocals, and negative conjugates
have a common feature in that they can be described by the general
double hyperbolic equation with asymptotes as
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"x")-axis and the lines ![y=x;
y=-x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y%3Dx%3B+y%3D-x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y=x; y=-x") (Figure 7),  
![y^4-x^2y^2-2y^2+1=0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y%5E4-x%5E2y%5E2-2y%5E2%2B1%3D0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"y^4-x^2y^2-2y^2+1=0")

[![Metallic\_ratio\_curve\_Fig7](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/metallic_ratio_curve_fig7.png?w=651&h=443)](https://manasataramgini.files.wordpress.com/2019/07/metallic_ratio_curve_fig7.png)Figure
7

They emerge as the solutions
![m\_n](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=m_n&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"m_n") of the biquadratic equation in
![y](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=y&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "y")
for the integer values of
![x](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=x&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0 "x"):  
![m\_n=\\dfrac{\\pm n \\pm
\\sqrt{n^2+4}}{2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=m_n%3D%5Cdfrac%7B%5Cpm+n+%5Cpm+%5Csqrt%7Bn%5E2%2B4%7D%7D%7B2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"m_n=\\dfrac{\\pm n \\pm \\sqrt{n^2+4}}{2}")

Thus, ![n=1 \\rightarrow \\phi; n=2 \\rightarrow \\sigma; n=3
\\rightarrow \\beta
...](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%3D1+%5Crightarrow+%5Cphi%3B+n%3D2+%5Crightarrow+%5Csigma%3B+n%3D3+%5Crightarrow+%5Cbeta+...&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"n=1 \\rightarrow \\phi; n=2 \\rightarrow \\sigma; n=3 \\rightarrow \\beta ...").
This also shows that they all follow the conjugate principle with
respect to their conjugates, i.e. ![m\_n-n=\\tfrac{1}{m\_n};
m\_n^2=1+nm\_n](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=m_n-n%3D%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7Bm_n%7D%3B+m_n%5E2%3D1%2Bnm_n&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0
"m_n-n=\\tfrac{1}{m_n}; m_n^2=1+nm_n"). As the double hyperbola
converges to its asymptotes the fractional parts of the ratios converge
to 0. Thus, it only the first few metallic ratios are likely to be
“interesting”. Indeed the Golden ratio is a common encountered in all
manner of mathematical situations. The Silver ratio is seen to a lesser
extant and the Bronze ratio is even rarer in it occurrence (conversely
to the metals after which they are named). Beyond the Bronze ratio these
ratios are mostly “uninteresting” in that they are rarely found in
mathematical entities.
